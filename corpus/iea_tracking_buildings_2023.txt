===============================================================
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY — BUILDINGS SECTOR 2023
===============================================================

The buildings sector remains off track for achieving net zero emissions
by mid-century. Despite progress in some areas, the pace of
decarbonization is insufficient to meet the IEA's Net Zero Emissions
by 2050 Scenario (NZE Scenario).

KEY FIGURES (2022 data):
- Buildings sector share of global final energy consumption: 30%
- Direct CO2 emissions from buildings operations: 3 Gt CO2 (26% of global)
- Indirect CO2 emissions (from electricity used in buildings): 6.8 Gt CO2
- Total buildings sector emissions (direct + indirect): approximately 10 Gt CO2
- Buildings account for over 40% of total energy-related emissions
  when including embodied carbon from construction materials.

ASSESSMENT: Buildings sector — "More efforts needed" (TCEP 2023)
Only Building Lighting received "On Track" status.

===============================================================
SECTION 1 — ENERGY CONSUMPTION TRENDS
===============================================================

1.1 GLOBAL ENERGY CONSUMPTION
Buildings consumed approximately 130 EJ of final energy in 2022.
This represents 30% of global total final energy consumption.

Breakdown by end use:
- Space heating: 42% of buildings energy consumption
- Water heating: 18%
- Lighting: 14%
- Cooling: 10%
- Cooking: 9%
- Appliances and other: 7%

1.2 REGIONAL DISTRIBUTION
China: largest buildings energy consumer globally (~25% of total)
United States: second largest (~20%)
European Union: ~15%
Developing Asia (ex-China): fastest growing region

1.3 FLOOR AREA GROWTH
Global buildings floor area grew by approximately 2.5% annually,
reaching approximately 235 billion m² in 2022.
By 2050, floor area is projected to reach 330 billion m².
This growth — concentrated in emerging economies — poses a
major challenge: new buildings being constructed today will
determine energy patterns for the next 50 years.

===============================================================
SECTION 2 — EMISSIONS AND CLIMATE TARGETS
===============================================================

2.1 CURRENT EMISSIONS STATUS
Direct CO2 emissions from buildings declined slightly in 2022
(approximately 3.0 Gt CO2) compared to 2021 peak.
However, this decline is insufficient — emissions must fall
by 50% from 2022 levels by 2030 to align with NZE Scenario.

2.2 NZE SCENARIO REQUIREMENTS
To reach net zero by 2050, the buildings sector must achieve:
- 50% reduction in operational emissions by 2030
- Zero-carbon-ready new buildings by 2030 in all markets
- Deep renovation of at least 2.5% of existing building stock annually
- Full electrification of space and water heating by 2030 in new buildings
- 25% reduction in embodied carbon from steel by 2030
- 20% reduction in embodied carbon from cement by 2030

2.3 EMISSIONS INTENSITY
Global operational emissions intensity:
- 2022: approximately 23 kgCO2/m² (global average)
- NZE 2030 target: approximately 12 kgCO2/m²
- NZE 2050 target: approximately 2 kgCO2/m²

===============================================================
SECTION 3 — KEY TECHNOLOGY AREAS
===============================================================

3.1 BUILDING ENVELOPES
Status: More efforts needed
Improving the thermal performance of building envelopes is the
single largest opportunity for reducing buildings energy demand.

Key metrics:
- Only about 1% of existing buildings undergo deep renovation annually
- Target: 2.5% annual renovation rate by 2030
- Energy savings potential from envelope upgrades: up to 70%

Technologies:
- High-performance insulation (EPS, XPS, PIR, mineral wool)
- Triple-pane windows (U-value ≤ 0.8 W/m²K)
- Phase-change materials for thermal mass
- Vacuum insulation panels (emerging)

3.2 SPACE HEATING AND COOLING
Status: More efforts needed

Heat pumps — key technology for decarbonization:
- Sales of heat pumps increased 11% in 2022 globally
- Still only represent ~12% of heating equipment sales
- Coefficient of Performance (COP): 2.5 to 5.0 (seasonal average)
- Heat pumps must reach 50% of all new heating sales by 2025 (NZE)

Air conditioning:
- Global cooling demand is rising rapidly (climate change)
- Minimum Energy Performance Standards (MEPS) critical
- Smart thermostats and demand response integration needed

3.3 LIGHTING
Status: ON TRACK (only sector in buildings reaching this status)
LED adoption exceeded 50% of global lighting stock in 2022.
LED lifetime: 15,000 to 50,000 hours vs 1,000 hours for incandescent
LED efficiency: 80 to 200 lm/W vs 10-15 lm/W for incandescent
Projected savings: LED transition could save 1,400 TWh globally by 2030.

3.4 APPLIANCES AND EQUIPMENT
Status: More efforts needed
Appliance energy standards must cover >80% of sales in all markets.
Key appliances for efficiency: refrigerators, washing machines, TVs.
Standby power remains a significant waste: ~1% of global electricity.

3.5 WATER HEATING
Solar thermal and heat pump water heaters are key alternatives
to fossil fuel and resistance electric water heating.
Heat pump water heaters: 3-4x more efficient than resistance heating.
Solar thermal collectors: displace 60-80% of water heating demand.

===============================================================
SECTION 4 — BUILDING ENERGY CODES AND STANDARDS
===============================================================

4.1 GLOBAL STATUS OF BUILDING CODES
Approximately 60% of countries have mandatory building energy codes.
However, only about 35% have codes that cover both residential and
commercial buildings comprehensively.

Key finding: Countries without mandatory codes are responsible for
a rapidly growing share of new construction.

4.2 ZERO-CARBON-READY BUILDINGS
Definition: A zero-carbon-ready building is highly energy efficient
and either uses low-carbon energy directly or is enabled to do so
with changes to the energy system.

Progress: Only 5 countries have adopted zero-carbon-ready building
standards for new construction: Norway, Denmark, Germany, Netherlands,
and France (EU directive-driven).

4.3 MINIMUM ENERGY PERFORMANCE STANDARDS (MEPS)
Appliance MEPS in place in countries representing >70% of global sales.
Building code MEPS adopted by countries representing ~60% of global floor area.

===============================================================
SECTION 5 — NET ZERO ENERGY BUILDINGS (NZEB)
===============================================================

5.1 DEFINITION
A Net Zero Energy Building (NZEB) produces as much energy from
renewable sources as it consumes on an annual basis.

Key elements:
- Ultra-low energy demand (typically <30 kWh/m²/year for space heating)
- High-performance building envelope (super-insulation, air-tightness)
- Efficient mechanical systems (heat recovery ventilation)
- On-site renewable energy generation (typically photovoltaic)
- Energy storage (battery or thermal) for demand flexibility

5.2 CURRENT STATUS
NZEBs represent less than 0.5% of global building stock.
However, with strong policies and falling renewable costs,
NZEBs are becoming economically viable in an increasing number
of markets, including in emerging economies.

5.3 PASSIVE HOUSE STANDARD
The Passive House (Passivhaus) standard is the leading NZEB framework:
- Heating demand: ≤ 15 kWh/m²/year
- Total primary energy: ≤ 120 kWh/m²/year
- Air leakage: ≤ 0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pa
- Over 65,000 certified buildings globally (2023)

===============================================================
SECTION 6 — RETROFITTING EXISTING BUILDINGS
===============================================================

6.1 IMPORTANCE OF RETROFITS
Existing buildings represent the greatest decarbonization challenge:
- 80% of buildings standing in 2050 already exist today
- Deep retrofits can reduce energy consumption by 50-80%
- Average building lifetime: 50-100 years
- High retrofit costs are the primary barrier

6.2 RETROFIT LEVELS
Shallow retrofit: simple measures — insulation top-up, LED lighting
(energy reduction: 10-30%, cost: $50-200/m²)

Medium retrofit: envelope improvements + system upgrades
(energy reduction: 30-50%, cost: $200-500/m²)

Deep retrofit: whole-building approach, near-NZEB performance
(energy reduction: 60-80%, cost: $500-1,500/m²)

6.3 FINANCING MECHANISMS
Green mortgages, on-bill financing, energy performance contracts (EPC),
and government incentive programs are key enablers.
IEA recommends combining minimum energy standards for existing buildings
with accessible financing to overcome the split incentive problem.

===============================================================
SECTION 7 — DIGITAL TECHNOLOGIES IN BUILDINGS
===============================================================

7.1 BUILDING AUTOMATION AND CONTROL SYSTEMS (BACS)
Smart building controls can reduce energy consumption by 10-30%
with minimal capital investment.
Key systems: smart thermostats, occupancy sensors, demand-controlled
ventilation, predictive heating/cooling algorithms.

7.2 DIGITAL TWINS
Building digital twins enable real-time monitoring and optimization
of energy performance.
Projected savings from digital optimization: 5-15% of building energy.

7.3 ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE
AI-based predictive control of HVAC and lighting systems:
- Occupancy prediction: 10-20% HVAC energy savings
- Grid interaction optimization: shifting flexible loads to low-carbon periods
- Predictive maintenance: reducing equipment energy waste from degradation

===============================================================
SECTION 8 — POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS
===============================================================

IEA KEY POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS FOR BUILDINGS:

1. Mandatory zero-carbon-ready building codes for all new construction by 2025
2. Minimum energy performance standards for existing buildings
3. Strong financial incentives for deep retrofits
4. Accelerated adoption of heat pumps — phase-out of fossil fuel heating
5. Mandatory disclosure of building energy performance ratings
6. Carbon pricing to drive investment in low-carbon buildings
7. Skills and workforce development for green construction
8. Urban planning to support compact, transit-oriented development

"The buildings sector has some of the most cost-effective
decarbonization opportunities available. What is missing is
not technology but rather policy ambition and implementation."
— IEA World Energy Outlook 2023
